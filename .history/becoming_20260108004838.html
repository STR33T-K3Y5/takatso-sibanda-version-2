Becoming Dr. Example Name

Welcome to this space dedicated to exploring the journey of becoming—a process of growth, transformation, and self-discovery that continues throughout our lives. Here, I share my research explorations, personal reflections, and creative expressions that have shaped my path as a community psychologist and engaged scholar.

Research

My research journey has been fundamentally shaped by the communities I have had the privilege to work alongside. From my earliest experiences coordinating psychosocial support programs to my current doctoral research, I have been committed to understanding how communities define, experience, and promote wellbeing on their own terms.

ThePromoting Rights-Based Evaluation project represents a culmination of years of thinking about how evaluation can serve communities rather than external agendas. This work challenges traditional evaluation paradigms that prioritize funder requirements over community-defined indicators of success, proposing instead collaborative approaches that build local evaluation capacity while honoring diverse ways of knowing.

My ethnographic documentary work, particularly "Becoming Who I Am," emerged from a deep belief in the power of storytelling to transform how we understand ourselves and each other. By centering the narratives of individuals navigating complex identity negotiations, this work resists simplification and embraces the messiness of lived experience.

Currently, I am exploring how narrative inquiry can be utilized as both a research methodology and a community intervention, examining how the act of telling and sharing stories can contribute to individual and collective healing processes.

Reflection

Reflecting on my journey, I recognize how each experience has built upon the last, creating a tapestry of learning that continues to unfold. The transition from practice to doctoral research was not straightforward—it required unlearning much of what I had been taught about knowledge production and reimagining what it means to be a researcher committed to community wellbeing.

I have come to understand that my identity as a community psychologist is inseparable from my own experiences of marginalization and resilience. These lived experiences inform my research questions, my methodological choices, and my commitments to centering community voices. They remind me that academic knowledge is just one form of understanding, and that wisdom exists within communities often excluded from academic discourse.

My time at Michigan State University has challenged me to hold multiple tensions simultaneously: rigor and relevance, academic citizenship and community accountability, individual achievement and collective flourishing. Learning to navigate these tensions has been a profound growth experience, pushing me to develop a research practice that refuses false dichotomies.

Perhaps most importantly, reflection has taught me the value of humility in the research process. I am continually reminded that communities are not simply subjects of study but are experts in their own lives. My role is not to extract knowledge but to facilitate processes through which community wisdom can be recognized, valued, and shared.

Creative Writing

Creative expression has always been integral to my work and identity as a community psychologist. I believe that the arts offer unique capacities for engaging with complexity, ambiguity, and emotion in ways that traditional academic writing cannot. Through creative writing, I aim to make space for the aesthetic dimensions of community life and research practice.

My creative work often emerges from community storytelling projects, where I have had the honor of listening to and documenting community members' experiences. These narratives, transformed through poetic or prose rendering, honor the original speakers while reaching audiences who might not otherwise encounter these stories.

One ongoing creative project explores the concept of "becoming" through fragmented narrative and imagery, examining how identity is constructed through ongoing interactions with others and environments. This work resists linear narrative conventions, instead embracing the non-linear, contradictory nature of identity development.

I also create creative non-fiction pieces that blend personal experience with scholarly reflection, offering accessible entry points into complex concepts in community psychology. These pieces demonstrate how academic ideas can be communicated through narrative rather than technical prose, making scholarly work available to broader audiences.

About Me

My path to community psychology began through direct service work, where I witnessed both the profound resilience of communities facing systemic challenges and the limitations of service delivery models that failed to center community voice. These early experiences planted seeds of questioning that continue to guide my work today.

As a Black African scholar navigating academic institutions often not designed for people like me, I have developed a critical awareness of how knowledge production is structured by power relations. This awareness fuels my commitment to collaborative methodologies that challenge traditional researcher-subject dynamics and create space for diverse forms of expertise.

My creative practice and scholarly work are deeply intertwined, with each informing the other. The ethnographic documentary I created represents years of collaborative work with community members, exploring intersections of identity, embodiment, and community belonging through visual storytelling and narrative inquiry.

I am committed to building communities of practice that bridge academic and community knowledge, creating spaces where researchers, practitioners, and community members can learn from each other. This commitment extends to my mentorship of emerging scholars, particularly those from marginalized backgrounds pursuing careers in community psychology.

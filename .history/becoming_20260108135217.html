Becoming Dr. Sibanda

Welcome! I’m glad you’re here. In this space, I share my stories, reflections, and experiences as a PhD student — the growth, the lessons, and the becoming. I invite you to connect and walk with me on this journey of becoming Dr. Sibanda. Thanks for visiting.

About Me

I am a community psychologist whose research and practice center the wellbeing of communities. My work focuses on HIV/AIDS prevention and care, psychosocial support, sexual and reproductive health and rights (SRHR), gender‑based violence (GBV) support, and life‑skills development interventions across diverse groups and contexts.

I am currently a PhD student in Community Psychology at Michigan State University, where I contribute to research in the Research Consortium on Gender‑Based Violence and the HIV and Sexual Health Research Lab. My work centers people who occupy marginal spaces in society, and I am committed to developing wellness spaces where individuals can show up fully as themselves and receive quality, affirming, and inclusive services.

Outside of academic and professional spaces, I prioritize my own wellbeing with the same intentionality I bring to my work. I am deeply rooted in my home, Bulawayo, Zimbabwe, and my experiences across different places continue to shape how I understand identity, community, belonging, and the many ways people practice wellness. I enjoy walking, coloring, Zumba, art museums, reading, traveling, and spending quality time with loved ones. These practices provide the joy and rest that sustain me as I navigate graduate school.

I hope that my work and story inspire others to dream, expand their horizons, and imagine new possibilities for promoting wellness in all its forms.

Research Areas

The Made‑in‑Africa Evaluation Framework (MAE)

I explore decolonial approaches to thinking about and practicing evaluation as a tool for social justice and community‑driven transformative change. The Made‑in‑Africa Evaluation (MAE) Framework resonates with me because it honors Ubuntu, African ways of being, seeing, relating, and doing, as well as community priorities, agency and lived expertise. These principles guide how I understand evaluation as a relational, contextually grounded practice. My work examines the evolution of MAE and its core tenets, how MAE principles are operationalized in various settings and the development of practice guidelines to advance MAE.

Reflection

Reflecting on my journey, I recognize how each experience has built upon the last, creating a tapestry of learning that continues to unfold. The transition from practice to doctoral research was not straightforward—it required unlearning much of what I had been taught about knowledge production and reimagining what it means to be a researcher committed to community wellbeing.

I have come to understand that my identity as a community psychologist is inseparable from my own experiences of marginalization and resilience. These lived experiences inform my research questions, my methodological choices, and my commitments to centering community voices. They remind me that academic knowledge is just one form of understanding, and that wisdom exists within communities often excluded from academic discourse.

My time at Michigan State University has challenged me to hold multiple tensions simultaneously: rigor and relevance, academic citizenship and community accountability, individual achievement and collective flourishing. Learning to navigate these tensions has been a profound growth experience, pushing me to develop a research practice that refuses false dichotomies.

Perhaps most importantly, reflection has taught me the value of humility in the research process. I am continually reminded that communities are not simply subjects of study but are experts in their own lives. My role is not to extract knowledge but to facilitate processes through which community wisdom can be recognized, valued, and shared.

Creative Writing

Creative expression has always been integral to my work and identity as a community psychologist. I believe that the arts offer unique capacities for engaging with complexity, ambiguity, and emotion in ways that traditional academic writing cannot. Through creative writing, I aim to make space for the aesthetic dimensions of community life and research practice.

My creative work often emerges from community storytelling projects, where I have had the honor of listening to and documenting community members' experiences. These narratives, transformed through poetic or prose rendering, honor the original speakers while reaching audiences who might not otherwise encounter these stories.

One ongoing creative project explores the concept of "becoming" through fragmented narrative and imagery, examining how identity is constructed through ongoing interactions with others and environments. This work resists linear narrative conventions, instead embracing the non-linear, contradictory nature of identity development.

I also create creative non-fiction pieces that blend personal experience with scholarly reflection, offering accessible entry points into complex concepts in community psychology. These pieces demonstrate how academic ideas can be communicated through narrative rather than technical prose, making scholarly work available to broader audiences.

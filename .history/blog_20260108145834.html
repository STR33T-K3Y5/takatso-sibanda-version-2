Diversity in the Dollhouse: Navigating Identity and Imagination

When we think about the dolls and toys that shape our understanding of identity, we often overlook the profound ways these small objects carry cultural weight. The simple act of choosing which doll to play with—or which doll is available to play with—can send powerful messages about belonging, visibility, and worth. For children who rarely see themselves reflected in mainstream toys, the absence speaks louder than any words could. Representation matters not because it is a luxury, but because it is a fundamental human need to see oneself reflected in the world around us.

The dollhouse has long served as a microcosm of society, reflecting (and sometimes challenging) the social hierarchies of its time. When we examine the evolution of doll design, we see threads of changing attitudes toward race, gender, disability, and social class woven through decades of consumer culture. Yet progress has been uneven, with certain identities remaining visible while others remain marginalized or erased entirely. The current moment offers an opportunity to reconsider what we want our play spaces to communicate about who belongs and who matters.

Beyond mere representation, toys like dolls serve as tools for children to practice navigating complex social situations. Through imaginative play, children work through fears, hopes, and identity questions in ways that adults often underestimate. When a child plays with a doll that looks like them, they are not just pretending—they are rehearsing their place in the world, imagining possibilities, and constructing narratives about who they might become. This is why diversity in play matters so deeply.

Storytelling as Resistance: Narrative Methods in Community Research

By Takatso Sibanda December 2024 5 minutes read

In an era where data often dominates how we understand communities, there is something powerful about returning to the ancient practice of storytelling. Numbers can inform, but stories transform—they connect us to lived experience in ways that statistics cannot replicate.

In an era where data often dominates how we understand communities, there is something powerful about returning to the ancient practice of storytelling. Numbers can inform, but stories transform—they connect us to lived experience in ways that statistics cannot replicate. When we center narrative methods in community research, we are not simply choosing a methodology; we are making a statement about what counts as knowledge and whose knowledge counts.

The communities I work with have long understood the power of stories. Before I arrived with my academic frameworks, they were already gathering to share experiences, to bear witness to each other's struggles and triumphs, to create collective meaning from individual journeys. My role has been to learn from these existing practices and to find ways that academic research can serve rather than extract from community knowledge systems.

Using storytelling as a research method requires humility. It means being willing to sit with discomfort, to hear stories that challenge our assumptions, and to resist the urge to categorize and contain narratives that resist easy interpretation. It also requires creativity in how we document, analyze, and share stories in ways that honor their complexity while making them accessible to diverse audiences.

Storytelling can be an act of resistance against reductionist approaches that flatten human experience into data points. When community members tell their own stories in their own words, they assert their expertise over their own lives. Researchers who listen and amplify these stories become allies in a broader project of social recognition and justice.

The Ethics of Care in Community-Based Research

By Takatso Sibanda November 2024 4 minutes read

Ethical research practice extends far beyond informed consent and IRB approval. In community-based work, ethics is an ongoing practice of care, relationship, and accountability that continues long after data collection ends.

Ethical research practice extends far beyond informed consent and IRB approval. In community-based work, ethics is an ongoing practice of care, relationship, and accountability that continues long after data collection ends. The questions we ask, how we ask them, and what we do with the answers all carry ethical weight that requires constant reflection and dialogue with community partners.

I have learned that ethical community research requires us to slow down. The pressure to produce publishable findings quickly can lead to extraction rather than reciprocity. Taking time to build relationships, to understand community priorities, and to ensure that research serves community needs requires patience and a willingness to let go of researcher-centered timelines and agendas.

Care ethics also demands that we consider the emotional labor we ask of community members. Sharing stories of struggle and resilience is hard work. Researchers must be thoughtful about how we create space for this sharing, how we honor the vulnerability people offer, and how we ensure that participation does not cause harm or re-traumatization.

Finally, ethics requires ongoing accountability. This means sharing research findings with communities in accessible formats, involving community members in interpretation, and considering how research can be used to support community advocacy and change efforts. True ethical practice sees community members as partners rather than participants.
